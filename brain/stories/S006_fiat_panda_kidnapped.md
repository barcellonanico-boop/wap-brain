## Story Body

April 2023. Tuesday morning. I'm trying to leave my apartment. That's it. That's the whole goal. Leave the apartment.

I live on Via Maqueda. The main pedestrian street of Palermo. Pedestrian. You know what that means? No cars. Big signs. Pictures of a little guy walking. Universal symbol. A caveman would understand it.

There's a gate to my building. In front of the gate, another sign. "Do not park." Do. Not. Park. Three words. Italian, English, probably Latin too for all I know.

I go downstairs at eight in the morning. Cup of coffee, keys in hand, ready to start my day like a functioning human being.

I push the gate.

Nothing.

I push harder.

Nothing.

I squeeze my face through the bars like a guy in prison, and I look.

A Fiat Panda. Six inches from my gate. SIX INCHES. He didn't just park there, he kissed my building. This car is spooning my gate.

I call the police. 8:05.

"We'll send a patrol when one is available."

Great. Thank you. When one is available. What does that mean? Tomorrow? Next week? When the Pope dies? I don't know.

8:30. I call again.

Different guy. This one goes, "Signore, there's a marathon in the city today, we're kind of busy."

A marathon.

In Palermo.

A MARATHON.

In a city where people take the scooter to go fifteen meters to buy cigarettes, there's a marathon. Who's running? Who woke up this morning and said, "You know what I want to do today? Run. For fun. In Palermo." Nobody. Nobody is running. But okay. Marathon. Fine.

Nine o'clock. I call again.

"Five minutes, we'll be there."

Five minutes.

You know what five minutes means in Sicily? It means never. It means forget about it. It means go make lunch, take a nap, grow a beard, five minutes is not a real unit of time here. Five minutes is a feeling.

Forty minutes later, no cops.

9:45. I call again.

"We'll do a round in the area."

A round. He's gonna do a round. Like it's a boxing match. Like the cops are gonna swing by, touch the car, ding ding, round over, go back to the station.

Six calls. Seven. I lost count. Five hours. FIVE HOURS I'm in my own building, pacing, calling, looking through the gate at this Panda like it's my hostage taker.

At one o'clock I hear keys jingling outside.

I fly down the stairs.

The guy is moving his car. Not because the cops came. The cops never came. The cops are still doing their "round," somewhere in a parallel universe. This guy came back because HE was done. On his own schedule. La la la.

I look at him. I say, "Are you fucking kidding me?"

And this guy. This guy. Swear to God. He looks me in the eye and he says:

"It was just two minutes."

TWO MINUTES.

Two minutes. Brother. I called the police six times. I watched the sun move across the sky. I missed two appointments. My coffee went cold. My second coffee went cold. I considered what my life choices had led me to. TWO MINUTES?

And I want you to understand something. In Sicily, it's ALWAYS two minutes. Every illegal thing that happens here, when you confront the guy, the first words out of his mouth: "It was just two minutes."

The car blocking the ambulance? Two minutes.

The scooter on the pedestrian street going eighty? Two minutes.

The guy double-parked in front of the hospital? Two minutes.

The tourists who have been eating at the table in the middle of the road for three hours? You got it. Two minutes.

If you could put all the "two minutes" end to end, you'd have a third millennium.

I didn't argue. I don't argue anymore. You argue with these people, they look at you like you're the one with the problem. "What are you getting upset for? It was two minutes." Best case you waste your breath. Worst case you're in a fistfight with a guy over a parking spot, and guess who the cops finally show up for? Me. The guy who wanted to leave his house.

So here's the punchline of the whole thing.

I could sit here and tell you Sicilians are slobs. Sicilians don't respect the rules. It's the Sicilians, it's the Sicilians. Every travel blog says it with a little wink. "Oh, it's chaotic, but charming."

It's bullshit.

The guy with the Panda knew one thing. ONE thing. Nobody was coming. No cop, no ticket, no tow truck. He parked there because he could. Give that guy a ticket every time he does it and watch how fast his two minutes becomes zero minutes.

We're all the same. Italian, French, German, American. Take away the rules, we all become the guy in the Panda.

The reason Palermo looks like a cartoon is not the people. It's the guys who get paid to enforce the rules sitting in a bar somewhere eating a cornetto, while I'm upstairs in my apartment staring at a Fiat Panda that was "just two minutes."
