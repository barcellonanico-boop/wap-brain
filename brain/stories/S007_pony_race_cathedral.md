## Story Body

I want to tell you about my Tuesday.

I'm walking past the Cattedrale di Palermo. The cathedral. UNESCO. Normans. Kings buried inside. Tourists everywhere.

I look up, and in the street, right in front of the cathedral…

A pony race.

Two ponies. Two kids. One of them, I'm not exaggerating, seven years old. Seven. On a pony. Racing. In front of the cathedral. On a Tuesday.

I stopped walking. I thought I was having a stroke.

No permit. No festival. No ambulance. No cops. Nothing. Just ponies, kids, and a crowd of adults cheering like it was the Palio di Siena.

Where were the police?

That's a trick question. In Palermo, the question is never "where are the police." The question is, "are there police?"

Let me explain how this works.

The cops in Palermo? Ghosts. You don't see them. They don't walk. They don't patrol. You could ride an elephant down Via Maqueda and nobody would stop you. They might put it on Instagram, they wouldn't stop it.

Where ARE they, then?

Option one: a bar. There's one on Via Emerico Amari by the Politeama. Any hour, any day, three police cars out front, four cops inside eating cornettos. These guys have more time for cappuccino than I have for my own life.

Option two: the condo meeting.

You know what a condo meeting is? In Italy, everyone in the building shows up, argues for three hours, nothing gets fixed. That's the national sport. The cops adopted it.

When the newspapers complain enough, suddenly five cops appear at Piazza Verdi. Standing in a circle. Talking about soccer. One guy is on Instagram. I have the photos. My phone is a museum.

Now here's the punchline.

The cops are not just lazy. They're cowards.

Who do they fine? The kid on the scooter with no helmet? No. The guy selling fake Gucci in the middle of the street? No. The seven-year-old on a pony at the cathedral? Obviously not.

They fine the grandma who parked two inches over the line in Politeama.

Because the grandma says "I'm sorry" and pays. The kid on the scooter argues. The Gucci guy runs. The pony kid has a parent who will threaten to kill you. So the cops go fine the grandma. Easy. Done. Back to the bar.

One hundred meters from my apartment there's a restaurant called Battiloro. Good people, rules followed to the minute. Live music on weekends. You know when they can start playing? Sunday, 4pm. Not 3:59. 4pm. One minute early, the cops teleport in.

One hundred meters further, in the Vucciria, there are places without a license blasting music from breakfast until dawn. Seven days a week. No paperwork, no rules, no problem.

The cops never go to the Vucciria.

Why would they? The Vucciria has people who argue. Battiloro has people who pay.

Meanwhile the city hall keeps saying, "We don't have enough officers."

Italy: 398 cops per 100,000 people. EU average: 318. Germany: 301. USA: 240. Finland: 136, and their streets are cleaner than my kitchen.

Italy is the most policed country in Europe. We don't have a shortage. We have a shortage of cops who want to work.

Which brings me to the part I really can't explain.

No cops. No rules. No consequences. A seven-year-old racing a pony at the cathedral. And somehow, Palermo is SAFE. Nobody's stabbing anybody. Tourists walk home at 2am eating panelle. Nothing happens.

How?

I don't know. Maybe Palermitans are secretly the most well-behaved people on Earth. Maybe humans without rules aren't actually monsters. Maybe the criminals are so bored by useless cops they gave up.

What I know is this. Next time somebody tells you Palermo is chaotic because Sicilians have no discipline, send them to the cathedral on a Tuesday. Then ask them to find the cops.

It's not the Sicilians.

It's the guys standing in the circle.
