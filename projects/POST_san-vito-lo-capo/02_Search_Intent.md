## 2. User Profiling — Point A to Point B

### Point A — Where the user is

**Problem they're trying to solve:**
She is 3-4 months out from a Sicily trip and has heard San Vito Lo Capo is a famous beach. She doesn't know if it's worth a stop, how to fit it into her itinerary, or whether to base there or day-trip from Palermo.

**Frustrations / pains / fears:**
- Wasting precious vacation days on a beach that turns out to be over-touristed and underwhelming.
- Going in the wrong week (August) and getting price-gouged + crammed.
- Getting trapped in tourist-trap restaurants with bad food at inflated prices.
- Booking the wrong accommodation (too far from the beach, no parking, noisy).
- Logistics nightmare from Palermo (no direct train, unclear bus schedules, fear of driving Italian roads).

**Perceived barriers:**
- "I don't know how to get there from Palermo without renting a car."
- "I don't know if it's better as a day-trip or as a base."
- "Italian beach pricing is opaque to me — what's a fair lido price vs a rip-off?"
- "I'm reading 8 articles and they all say the same generic things."

**Knowledge gaps:**
- What the village actually FEELS like off the beach (do you have anything to do at night? Is there a town centre worth walking?)
- Riserva dello Zingaro — what it is, how to access, what to bring.
- The Couscous Festival in September — is it a reason to time the trip there or a reason to avoid?
- Egadi Islands as a day-trip from San Vito — feasible? worthwhile?
- 2026 prices: hotel range, lido, parking, restaurants.
- Common typos and variants she's been mis-spelling.

### Point B — Where they want to arrive

**Final desire:**
A clear, opinion-led decision on whether to include San Vito in her itinerary, how many days, where to stay (or whether to day-trip from Palermo), and exactly what to do once there — at 2026 prices, with the bad weeks called out so she can avoid them.

**Transformation sought:**
From "anxious comparison shopper reading her 6th San Vito article and getting nothing new" to "confident planner who has read THIS article and now knows the move." She wants to stop researching and start booking.

**Concrete outcome from this article:**
A booked accommodation (or a confirmed day-trip plan), a date selected with seasonality understood, 2-3 specific restaurants saved, and a clear sense of what the village will feel like when she arrives.

### The Bridge

This article must be the LAST San Vito article she reads, not the first. To do that it has to outperform the SERP top-10 on three dimensions: (a) decisive seasonal opinion that other guides won't give (brutal honesty about August), (b) Palermo-route logistics with concrete numbers (the +140% QoQ cluster), and (c) WAP-original recommendations on hotels/tours/restaurants — Nico's actual picks with reasons, not affiliate-grade aggregator content. The bridge is brutality + specificity + insider authority. Generic listicle tone fails the bridge because Google AIO already serves generic listicles.

## 3. Reader Thought Flow

10 questions in natural psychological order:

1. **Is this place actually worth my limited Sicily days?** — The reader's first gate. Without a "yes" here, she leaves. Bridges A→B by addressing the wasting-vacation fear before any logistics.

2. **When NOT to go?** — Logically follows the worth-it answer because seasonality determines whether her specific window is the right window. Bridges A→B by removing the August trap fear early.

3. **Should I base in San Vito or day-trip from Palermo?** — The single highest-leverage decision; everything downstream (hotel, restaurants, evenings) depends on it. Bridges A→B by collapsing two parallel planning tracks into one.

4. **How do I get there from Palermo (and how long does it take)?** — Logistics. The +140% QoQ cluster. Once the day-trip vs base question is decided, the next concrete obstacle is transportation. Bridges A→B by turning a fear (Italian roads, no train) into a numbered plan.

5. **What's the village actually like — beyond the beach?** — She needs to picture her evening: is there a lungomare? cafés? a town centre worth walking after dinner? +47-200% QoQ recovery on this cluster shows the demand is rising. Bridges A→B by giving her a sensory mental image of the place.

6. **What about the beach — free vs lido, prices, crowding by month?** — The core attraction with concrete 2026 numbers. Bridges A→B by removing the lido-pricing opacity barrier.

7. **What about Riserva dello Zingaro and other natural-side activities?** — The reader who got past the beach question now wants the secondary draws. Bridges A→B by giving her a one-day-extension reason to stay rather than day-trip.

8. **Where do I eat? (3-5 actual names, 2026 prices)** — Restaurants are emerging cluster (+30% QoQ, +200-300% YoY on long-tail). Bridges A→B by removing the tourist-trap fear with names.

9. **Where do I sleep? (Persona A €100-200/night range)** — Booking-stage question. Currently 100% gap in WAP_12 — Phase 5 dependency. Bridges A→B by completing the booking-decision chain.

10. **What's nearby that's worth pairing? (Egadi day-trip, Couscous Festival timing, Trapani/Erice)** — Last-mile detail that turns one article into the LAST article. Bridges A→B by extending the trip plan beyond just San Vito.

**Order rationale:**
The order follows the psychological arc of a planner moving from doubt → decision → logistics → sensory imagining → cost reality → activities → meals → bed → enrichment. Each question only makes sense once the previous is answered. Question 1 (worth it?) is the gate; it must be answered with brutal opinion or the rest is wasted. Question 3 (base vs day-trip) is the structural fork; placing it before logistics rather than after lets the logistics question (4) inherit the right framing (round-trip same-day vs one-way overnight). Question 5 (village character) is intentionally placed before beach detail (6) because it answers the "but what will my evenings feel like" sensory question that Persona A asks subconsciously before she commits to a hotel. Restaurants (8) and sleep (9) come late because they're action-stage decisions, not research-stage ones — the reader books those after she's bought into the trip.
